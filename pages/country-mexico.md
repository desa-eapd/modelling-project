<p><strong>When:</strong> 2017–2023</p>

<p><strong>Purpose:</strong> The project was structured around three CLEWs components, developed at different stages and with different partners:<br>
(1) rational <strong>water planning</strong> in central Mexico using the WEAP model<br>
(2) assessment of the potential of Mexico’s <strong>main crops and their water requirements</strong> <strong>under current &amp; future climate scenarios</strong> using the AEZ model<br>
(3) pathway analysis for <strong>renewable energy transitions</strong> using the OSeMOSYS model</p>

<p><strong>Context:</strong> As a developing country with moderate levels of emissions, Mexico has made a commitment to redress climate change and move towards sustainable development, including by passing a General Climate Change Law. Following the ratification of the Paris Agreement, the finance &amp; environment ministries requested support to build capacities in the use of modelling tools &amp; integrated assessment methodologies to address interlinkages &amp; trade-offs among goals, priorities, and sectoral policies.</p>

<p><strong>Key insights:</strong></p>

<ul>
  <li>The agricultural analysis showed the potential impacts climate change on crop suitability, yields, and water requirements across regions, highlighting the <strong>increased vulnerability in water-scarce &amp; temperature-sensitive areas</strong>.</li>
</ul>

![Mexico CLEWs project image](../../assets/images/country-projects/mexico-1.png)

<ul>
  <li>The energy analysis showed that Mexico’s power system (currently heavily reliant on natural gas) can <strong>transition toward a high share of renewables over the long term</strong> thanks to declining technology costs, though with implications for investment needs and system integration.</li>
</ul>

<p><strong>Tools:</strong> Water Evaluation and Planning (WEAP), Agro-Ecological Zones (AEZ), Open Source Energy Modelling Systems (OSeMOSYS).</p>

<p><strong>Stakeholders:</strong> The Government of Mexico’s Secretary of Environment &amp; Natural Resources (SEMARNAT), National Institute for Ecology &amp; Climate Change (INECC), Energy Secretariat (SENER), UN DESA, UNDP, ECLAC, and IIASA.</p>
