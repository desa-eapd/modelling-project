**When:** 2025, building on earlier CLEW case-study work

**Purpose:** Strengthen national capacity in a small-island context to conduct integrated food, energy, water, and climate analysis and use it for policy coherence, resilience, and green-economy planning.

**What happened:** The 2025 Mauritius CLEWs project was designed around online pre-training, model updating, in-person scenario analysis, and inter-institutional collaboration. Earlier CLEW work with FAREI and the IAEA examined sugarcane ethanol, drought-resistant crops, reduced precipitation, energy imports, and water withdrawals.

**Key insights:**

- Mauritius' nexus choices are shaped by structural import dependence: the case-study material reports that the country imports about 70% of its food needs and more than 80% of its energy needs.
- First-generation ethanol from sugarcane can reduce gasoline imports and greenhouse-gas emissions, but it increases water demand and electricity needs for irrigation and pumping.
- Second-generation ethanol can reduce gasoline use further, but diverting bagasse away from electricity generation can increase coal and distillate imports, offsetting part of the benefit.
- Under the worst-case climate scenario shown in the case study, 2030 water withdrawals for first-generation ethanol rise to roughly 370 million m3, about 40 million m3 above the reference pathway.
- Reduced precipitation makes rainfed sugarcane less suitable in northern and western areas, strengthening the case for crop choices and irrigation strategies that are robust under drought.
